Bauhaus (1919-1933)

1919-1933

Bauhaus organized design as Gesamtkunstwerk: a program linking architecture, craft, and communication. Founded in Weimar in 1919, it shifted toward industrial production and standardization by 1923, moved to Dessau in 1925, and closed in 1933 under political pressure. Its workshop model treated reduction and rationalism as methods, not style effects. Furniture, metalwork, and typography were designed for repeatable use, with modular proportion and functional typography supporting clarity. The movement's influence comes from aligning formal discipline with manufacturing constraints.

Gesamtkunstwerk Industrial production Standardization Reduction Rationalism

Examples: The Met Collection · MoMA Collection

Confusion: Not to be confused with Art Deco because Bauhaus used reduction and industrial production instead of ornamental luxury.

Accountability: I verified this by checking The Met, "The Bauhaus, 1919-1933".

International Typographic Style / Swiss Style (1950-1970)

1950-1970

Swiss Style, also called the International Typographic Style, consolidated after World War II and is often framed as 1950-1970. Its method prioritized the typographic grid, grotesque typeface choices, flush-left rag-right setting, and compositional asymmetry to maximize legibility and readability across languages. Designers in Zurich and Basel pursued universality through reduction, formal discipline, and functional typography, often replacing illustration with photography. This was rationalism in public communication: order, modular proportion, and repeatable systems. By the 1970s and 1980s, this model was challenged by structural experimentation.

Typographic grid Flush-left rag-right Grotesque typeface Universality Functional typography

Examples: MoMA Collection (No. 4, 1957) · MoMA Collection (Less is More)

Confusion: Not to be confused with Minimalism because Swiss Style depends on typographic grid, flush-left rag-right, and functional typography rather than reduction alone.

Accountability: I verified this by checking Swiss National Library, "The International style 1950-1970".

New Wave Typography (Late 1960s-1980s)

Late 1960s-1980s

New Wave Typography emerged as a reaction to Swiss formal discipline in the late 1960s and spread through the 1970s-1980s. Wolfgang Weingart questioned rigid grid rules and pushed structural experimentation that treated type as image, not only text. The result was spatial fragmentation, typographic distortion, and typographic contrast while negotiating legibility and readability. In the United States, April Greiman became a leading figure after relocating to Los Angeles in 1976. This movement did not reject rationalism; it redirected it toward expressive systems with complexity and variable rhythm.

Structural experimentation Spatial fragmentation Typographic distortion Typographic contrast Legibility

Examples: MoMA Collection (Weingart) · SFMOMA Collection (Greiman)

Confusion: Not to be confused with Grunge typography because New Wave used structural experimentation and typographic distortion within formal discipline, while Grunge often reduces readability.

Accountability: I verified this by checking Swiss National Library, "Postmodern posters, 1970 to today" and Cooper Hewitt, "April Greiman".
